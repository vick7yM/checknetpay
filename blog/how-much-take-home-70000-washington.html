$70,000 Salary in Washington State: 2026 Take-Home Pay — What No One Tells You

Washington State has no personal income tax on wages, making your $70,000 salary one of the most efficient in the country. Your standard 2026 take-home is $2,225.88 per paycheck. But Washington has introduced a long-term care payroll deduction (WA Cares Fund) that many workers don't know about, and a 2022 capital gains tax that affects investors. Here's the complete, honest picture — including what might be missing from simpler calculators.

Your Core Washington State Paycheck

$70,000 per year, paid bi-weekly: $2,692.31 gross per paycheck. Washington State takes nothing from wages. Your deductions are purely federal:

📊 $70,000 Salary — Washington State — Single — Bi-weekly (2026)
  • Gross pay per paycheck: $2692.31
  • Federal income tax: -$252.69 (9.4% effective rate)
  • Social Security: -$166.92 (6.2%)
  • Medicare: -$39.04 (1.45%)
  • Washington State income tax on wages: $0 (no wage income tax 🎉)
  • Net take-home: $2233.65 per paycheck
💡 Washington's no-income-tax structure: Washington has never had a personal income tax on wages, and the state supreme court has repeatedly affirmed constitutional barriers to creating one. Unlike Nevada (constitutional prohibition) or Texas (constitutional ban), Washington's protection is based on court interpretation of the state constitution's uniformity clause — but it has proven equally durable in practice.

The WA Cares Fund: Washington's Hidden Payroll Deduction

Here's what many take-home calculators miss: Washington workers are generally subject to the WA Cares Fund — a state long-term care insurance program funded by a payroll deduction of 0.58% of gross wages.

On a $70,000 salary, this adds up to:

  • $406/year deducted from your pay
  • $15.62 per bi-weekly paycheck
  • Adjusts your real net to approximately $2,210.26 per paycheck if WA Cares applies to you

In exchange, you become eligible for up to $36,500 in long-term care benefits (indexed to inflation) after meeting vesting requirements. The program is designed to provide a basic safety net for long-term care needs — nursing homes, in-home care, assisted living — which can otherwise cost $50,000–$100,000+ per year.

Who Is Exempt from WA Cares?

Workers who obtained an approved private long-term care insurance policy before November 1, 2021, and applied for an exemption from the Employment Security Department, are permanently exempt. If you missed that window, you're generally required to participate unless you meet specific exemption categories (self-employed who opt out, certain veterans with VA care coverage, non-immigrant visa holders, out-of-state residents working for a WA employer).

If you're not sure whether the deduction is hitting your pay stub, check your pay stubs carefully — it appears as a separate line item, not embedded in another deduction.

Washington's Capital Gains Tax: Doesn't Affect Your W-2 Paycheck

In 2022, Washington enacted a 7% tax on long-term capital gains above $250,000 per year. This was contentious — opponents argued it was an income tax prohibited by the constitution; the state supreme court ruled it valid as an excise tax.

For a W-2 worker earning $70,000 in wages, this has zero effect on your paycheck. The capital gains tax only applies to profits from selling stocks, bonds, and other assets above the $250,000 annual threshold. It does not apply to wages, salaries, retirement account distributions, or real estate sales of a primary residence. Mention it here because it's often cited in conversations about Washington's "no income tax" status — the nuance is that wages remain untaxed at the state level, while very high investment gains are not.

Washington vs. Oregon: The Border Effect

Washington and Oregon share the Columbia River as a border — and almost nothing else when it comes to taxation philosophy. Oregon has no sales tax but levies some of the highest income tax rates in the country (up to 9.9%). Washington has no income tax but collects significant sales tax.

The "Vancouver commuter" lifestyle has become a defined financial strategy in the Pacific Northwest.

What $70,000 Looks Like Across Washington State

Seattle

Seattle is one of the most expensive cities in the US, driven by tech sector salaries that have compressed the bottom of the housing market. A one-bedroom in Capitol Hill, Ballard, or Queen Anne runs $1,900–$2,600. Even further out — Beacon Hill, Rainier Beach, Northgate — one-bedrooms run $1,500–$1,900. At $70,000 ($4,452/month take-home for two paychecks, or $4,036 after WA Cares), Seattle on a solo income is difficult but doable with careful housing choices.

Seattle's transit system (Link Light Rail, bus rapid transit) is improving, which opens up more affordable neighborhoods outside the urban core. Workers who can live in Renton, Burien, or Kent and commute via transit can significantly reduce housing costs while maintaining Seattle-level salaries.

Bellevue / Eastside

The Eastside (Bellevue, Redmond, Kirkland, Bothell) hosts Microsoft, Amazon offices, and major tech employers. Housing is even more expensive than Seattle proper — one-bedrooms in Bellevue run $2,100–$2,800. At $70,000, the Eastside is very tight for a single income. Most workers earning at this level on the Eastside either commute from further out or have a dual income.

Tacoma

Tacoma has positioned itself as the affordable alternative to Seattle — 35 miles south on I-5, with Sounder train service providing a car-free commute option. One-bedrooms run $1,200–$1,600. Tacoma has invested in downtown revitalization and has a genuine arts and restaurant scene. At $70,000, Tacoma is one of the best value propositions in the Puget Sound region — big-city amenities accessible, housing still reasonable.

Spokane

Eastern Washington's largest city exists in a different economic reality from the Puget Sound metro. One-bedrooms run $900–$1,300. Healthcare, education, and logistics are major employers. At $70,000 in Spokane, you're earning well above the local median and can build substantial savings while maintaining a comfortable lifestyle. The tradeoff: fewer tech and professional services opportunities, and the job market is more limited if you need to change employers.

Vancouver (Clark County)

As noted, Vancouver has grown into the "Portland without Oregon taxes" destination. One-bedrooms run $1,200–$1,600 — cheaper than Portland proper. At $70,000, Vancouver + a Portland-area job is a compelling combination: Oregon salaries (which tend to be higher than Clark County local salaries) minus Oregon income tax.

Pre-Tax Savings in Washington

With no state income tax to reduce, Washington workers' pre-tax benefits create savings only at the federal level — but those savings are still real and meaningful:

  • 5% 401(k) ($3,500/year): At the 22% marginal bracket for $3,500 in the 22% zone and 12% for the rest, saves approximately $468 in federal tax. Net cost to you: $3,032 to invest $3,500.
  • HSA ($4,300/year): Triple tax advantage — deduction, growth, and withdrawals for medical expenses all tax-free. Saves ~$637 in federal tax at this income level.

FAQ

How do I know if I'm subject to WA Cares Fund?

If you're a W-2 employee in Washington State and didn't obtain a private LTC insurance exemption before November 2021, the deduction should be appearing on your pay stub. If you work for a Washington employer but live out of state, you may be exempt. Check your most recent pay stub for a "WA Cares" or "LTC" line item.

Does Seattle have a city income tax?

Seattle attempted to pass a city income tax in 2017; it was struck down by courts. As of 2026, no Washington city levies a personal income tax on wages.

Is $70,000 enough to live in Seattle?

For a single person without roommates, $70,000 in Seattle is tight given current rents. With a roommate, it's workable. With remote work flexibility to choose a more affordable neighborhood or nearby city (Tacoma, Renton, Lynnwood), it becomes genuinely comfortable. Seattle's no-income-tax advantage is partially offset by its high cost of living — but that cost of living is driven by competition for housing from workers earning $120,000–$200,000+ at tech companies, not by taxes.
