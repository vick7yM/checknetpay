How Much Do You Take Home Making $80,000 in Texas?

If you earn $80,000 a year in Texas, here's exactly what you'll take home after federal tax and FICA in 2026 — and how your paycheck compares to the same salary in high-tax states.

Your $80,000 Texas Salary — The Quick Answer

On a $80,000 annual salary in Texas, your bi-weekly take-home pay in 2026 is approximately $2,496.07 — or about $64,898 per year after taxes. That's without any pre-tax deductions like 401(k) or health insurance.

💡 Key fact: At $80,000, you've crossed into the 22% federal marginal bracket — but your effective federal rate is still only 11.2%, because only the income above $64,575 is taxed at 22%. Texas's $0 state tax makes your full gross pay highly efficient.

Texas is one of 9 states with no state income tax — see how federal and state taxes interact everywhere else.

Exact Tax Breakdown for $80,000 in Texas

📊 $80,000 Salary — Texas — Single — Bi-weekly
  • Gross pay per paycheck: $3,076.92
  • Federal income tax: -$345.08 (11.2% effective rate)
  • Social Security: -$190.77 (6.2%)
  • Medicare: -$44.62 (1.45%)
  • Texas state tax: $0 (no state income tax 🎉)
  • Net take-home: $2,496.45 per paycheck

How Federal Income Tax Is Calculated

The US uses a progressive tax bracket system. This means different portions of your income are taxed at different rates — not your entire salary at one flat rate.

For a single filer earning $80,000 in 2026:

  • First $16,100 is your standard deduction — taxed at 0%
  • $16,100–$28,025 taxed at 10% = $1,192
  • $28,025–$64,575 taxed at 12% = $4,386
  • $64,575–$80,000 taxed at 22% = $3,394
  • Total federal tax: $8,972 (11.2% effective rate)

$80,000 in Texas — What That Means Day to Day

At $80,000 in Texas, your bi-weekly paycheck of roughly $2,496 gives you about $4,992/month in take-home. That's a comfortable income in most Texas metros. In Austin, where housing costs have climbed sharply, you'd want to keep rent under $1,500 to stay under 30% of take-home. In Houston, Dallas, or San Antonio, the same salary typically stretches further — median rent in those cities generally runs $200–$500 lower per month than in Austin.

Texas does offset the income tax savings with relatively high property taxes (among the nation's highest effective rates), which matter if you own or plan to buy — but for renters, the paycheck advantage over high-tax states is nearly unfiltered.

Add 401(k) and Save More

If you contribute 5% of your $80,000 salary to a 401(k), that's $4,000 per year coming out pre-tax. This reduces your federal taxable income — saving about $880 in federal tax per year (at the 22% marginal rate) while building retirement savings.

Texas vs. California — The Difference

On the same $80,000 salary, a California resident pays approximately $7,440 more per year in state income tax than a Texas resident. Over 10 years, that's $74,400 in additional taxes — a number that explains the consistent net migration from California to Texas cities.
