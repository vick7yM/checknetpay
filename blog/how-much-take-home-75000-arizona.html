$75,000 Salary in Arizona: 2026 Take-Home Pay With the Nation's Lowest Flat Tax

Arizona made history by consolidating its income tax into the lowest flat rate of any state that taxes income — 2.5%. At $75,000, this means paying just $1,875 per year in state income tax, or $72.12 per paycheck. Your total 2026 take-home: $2,296.83 per bi-weekly paycheck, or $59,718 per year. Here's the full breakdown and what it means for workers across Arizona's fast-growing cities.

Your Arizona Paycheck: Complete 2026 Breakdown

$75,000 per year paid bi-weekly gives you $2,884.62 gross per paycheck. Arizona takes a modest slice; federal takes more:

  • Federal income tax: -$295.00 (10.2% effective rate)
  • Social Security: -$178.85 (6.2%)

💡 How Arizona got to 2.5%: Arizona previously had a multi-bracket system with rates up to 4.5%. In 2021, legislation phased the rate down to a single flat rate, reaching 2.5% for tax year 2023 and remaining there. The rate applies to all Arizona taxable income. It's so low that the difference between Arizona and zero-income-tax states is smaller than most people's monthly streaming subscriptions relative to their paycheck — $72.12 per check vs. $0 is real, but it's the smallest state tax bite of any taxing state in the US.

Federal Tax at $75,000: Entering the 22% Bracket

Your federal taxable income is $58,900 ($75,000 minus $16,100 standard deduction). This spans three brackets:

  • 10% on first $12,400: $1,240.00
  • 12% on $12,400–$50,400: $4,560.00
  • 22% on $50,400–$58,900: $1,870.00
  • Annual federal tax: $7,670 ($295.00/paycheck, 10.2% effective rate)

At $75,000, you have $8,500 of taxable income sitting in the 22% bracket. This is the first income level where 22% starts having a meaningful impact on your marginal math. Pre-tax contributions become particularly powerful here: every $1,000 contributed to a 401(k) saves $220 in federal tax (22 cents on the dollar), plus $25 in Arizona state tax (2.5 cents) — $245 in combined annual savings per $1,000 invested.

What $75,000 Looks Like in Arizona's Key Markets

Phoenix (Maricopa County)

Phoenix is the economic engine of Arizona and one of the fastest-growing metros in the US. The city's job market has diversified well beyond tourism and real estate into semiconductors, financial services, tech, and healthcare. One-bedroom apartments in popular areas (Arcadia, Biltmore, Tempe, Scottsdale Road corridor) run $1,400–$2,000. Further out (Glendale, Mesa, Chandler, Gilbert) brings that to $1,200–$1,600. At $75,000 ($4,594/month take-home for two paychecks), Phoenix is comfortable — particularly in the suburbs where the money goes furthest.

Phoenix's primary household budget challenge isn't taxes — it's electricity costs. Arizona's extreme heat (summer highs of 110°F+) means AC bills of $200–$400/month in summer are normal. Budget for this explicitly; new residents are regularly caught off-guard by the first summer electric bill.

Scottsdale

Scottsdale is Arizona's premium market — resort economy, medical tourism, and tech employers have created a high-cost pocket. One-bedrooms in Old Town Scottsdale or South Scottsdale run $1,700–$2,400. At $75,000, Scottsdale solo living requires intentionality. North Scottsdale is even pricier. Workers at $75,000 who want the Scottsdale lifestyle often live in neighboring Tempe or Mesa and commute.

Tucson

Tucson is Arizona's most affordable significant city — one-bedrooms in good areas run $900–$1,300. The University of Arizona drives a portion of the economy, but Tucson also has defense (Raytheon, Davis-Monthan AFB), medical, and government employment. At $75,000, Tucson is excellent — your take-home provides a comfortable lifestyle with genuine savings capacity. The summer heat is comparable to Phoenix but Tucson's higher elevation (2,400 feet vs. 1,100 feet) softens it slightly.

Flagstaff

At 7,000 feet elevation, Flagstaff has four genuine seasons and is beloved by outdoor enthusiasts. Proximity to Grand Canyon, skiing at Arizona Snowbowl, and year-round hiking make it unlike any other Arizona city. But housing is expensive relative to income — a university town effect, compounded by limited land. One-bedrooms run $1,200–$1,700. At $75,000, Flagstaff is doable but not comfortable solo, particularly given Flagstaff's higher utility costs (heating in winter, cooling much less than Phoenix).

Arizona's Real Estate Market: Should You Buy at $75,000?

Arizona's housing market has experienced significant appreciation — median home prices in the Phoenix metro now exceed $400,000. At $75,000, the conventional 3x income guideline suggests looking at homes around $225,000, which is at the very low end of the Phoenix market. FHA loans (3.5% down, lower credit score requirements) make homeownership accessible, but at current interest rates, a $300,000 home with 5% down produces a monthly payment of $2,000–$2,300 including property tax and insurance — roughly 56% of take-home pay for a single $75,000 earner. Most financial advisors would consider this too high. At $75,000 in Arizona, continuing to rent while aggressively saving for a larger down payment or waiting for salary growth may be the more sustainable path.

FAQ

Does Arizona have local income taxes?

No. No Arizona city or county levies a separate income tax. Your 2.5% flat state rate is the complete Arizona income tax obligation, regardless of where in the state you work or live.

Will Arizona's 2.5% rate change?

It's set in statute, not the constitution, meaning the legislature could theoretically increase it. However, the political momentum has been strongly toward lower taxes in Arizona, and no credible movement to raise the rate exists as of 2026. The more likely scenario is the rate stays flat or potentially decreases further if economic conditions allow.

How does Arizona's 2.5% compare to living in Nevada (no tax)?

At $75,000, the pure tax difference is $1,875/year. Nevada's sales tax (8.375% in Clark County) is significantly higher than Arizona's (5.6% state + local surtaxes, typically 8.1%–9.1% combined in Phoenix). Housing in Phoenix and Las Vegas are comparable. The income tax difference is real but modest enough that lifestyle preferences, job market, and specific city costs should drive the decision more than the $1,875 tax differential alone.
