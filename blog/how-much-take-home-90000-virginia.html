$90,000 Salary in Virginia: 2026 Take-Home Pay — Northern VA vs. The Rest of the State

Virginia has a progressive income tax that tops out at 5.75% — but that top rate kicks in at just $17,000 of Virginia taxable income, meaning almost every dollar above a modest threshold is taxed at the maximum rate. On $90,000, your 2026 take-home is $2,596.70 per bi-weekly paycheck, or $67,514 per year. The bigger variable: whether you work in Northern Virginia (where housing costs rival the most expensive metros in the country) or elsewhere in the state (where $90,000 provides genuine financial comfort).

Virginia's structure means nearly all of a $90,000 earner's income is taxed at the top rate — there are no "lower bracket savings" for middle-income earners the way there are at the federal level or in states like California with truly progressive systems.

How Virginia State Tax Is Calculated at $90,000

Virginia's standard deduction for a single filer is $8,500 in 2026. Your Virginia taxable income: $81,500 ($90,000 minus $8,500). Virginia's brackets:

  • 2% on first $3,000: $60.00
  • 3% on $3,000–$5,000: $60.00
  • 5% on $5,000–$17,000: $600.00
  • 5.75% on $17,000–$81,500: $3,708.75
  • Annual Virginia tax: $4,428.75 ($170.34/paycheck)

The effective Virginia rate is approximately 4.92% of gross salary — but because the top rate engages so early, nearly all of a $90,000 earner's Virginia-taxable income is at 5.75%. There is very little progressive "relief" from the lower brackets for anyone earning above $25,000 or so.

Northern Virginia vs. The Rest of Virginia: Two Different Realities

Virginia's geography creates two dramatically different financial experiences at the same income level:

Northern Virginia (Arlington, Alexandria, Fairfax, Loudoun, Prince William)

NoVA is economically tied to the DC metro — federal government, defense contracting, technology, and consulting drive the job market and the cost of living. One-bedroom apartments in Arlington, Old Town Alexandria, or Reston/Herndon run $1,900–$2,800. Tysons Corner, McLean, and Great Falls push even higher. Your $5,193/month take-home (two paychecks) against $2,300 rent leaves $2,893 for everything else — tight for the area's generally high cost of dining, transportation, and lifestyle. Most $90,000 earners in NoVA either:

  • Live in outer Prince William County or Loudoun County where one-bedrooms run $1,500–$2,000 but commute times on I-66 or Route 7 can exceed 60–90 minutes
  • Use Metro (the DC subway, which extends deep into Virginia) to reach more affordable areas like College Park or taking the VRE commuter rail
  • Have a second household income

NoVA's job market rewards patience. $90,000 is a solid entry to mid-career salary in defense contracting and federal government work — but the typical trajectory in that sector moves toward $110,000–$140,000 within 5–8 years for capable professionals, which changes the financial picture considerably.

Richmond

Virginia's state capital has transformed into a genuine mid-sized city with a vibrant food scene, strong craft brewery culture, and improving neighborhoods. One-bedrooms in desirable areas (The Fan, Carytown, Scott's Addition, Church Hill) run $1,200–$1,700. At $90,000 ($5,193/month take-home), Richmond provides excellent financial margin. Healthcare (VCU Health, HCA), financial services (Capital One), and state government are major employers. Richmond increasingly attracts DC-area workers who want quality of life with lower cost of living — $90,000 in Richmond is a meaningfully better financial position than $90,000 in NoVA.

Virginia Beach / Norfolk / Hampton Roads

The Hampton Roads region hosts significant military presence (Naval Station Norfolk, Langley AFB, Quantico) alongside a growing civilian economy. One-bedrooms in Virginia Beach and Norfolk run $1,200–$1,700. At $90,000, Hampton Roads is comfortable — above the regional median income with reasonable housing costs. The military and defense contracting ecosystem provides stable employment for $90,000-range professionals.

Charlottesville

Home to the University of Virginia, Charlottesville has become one of Virginia's most desirable smaller cities. Rents have climbed — one-bedrooms run $1,300–$1,800, elevated by university and medical center demand. At $90,000, Charlottesville is comfortable if you're in healthcare, education, or tech. The lifestyle (Blue Ridge Mountains access, wine country, university culture) is exceptional relative to cost.

Roanoke / Lynchburg / Blacksburg

Southwest and western Virginia offers dramatically lower costs. Roanoke one-bedrooms: $800–$1,200. Lynchburg: similar. Blacksburg (Virginia Tech home): $900–$1,300. At $90,000 in these cities, you're earning well above the local median and can build substantial financial security quickly. The job markets are more limited (healthcare, education, manufacturing, logistics), but for remote workers or those with established careers in these sectors, the financial leverage is significant.

Is Virginia's Tax Rate Likely to Change?

Virginia's 5.75% top bracket has been stable for many years. Unlike North Carolina (actively cutting) or Georgia (reduced its rate to a flat structure), Virginia has not pursued income tax reduction as a legislative priority under recent administrations. The DC suburb economic dynamic — where the federal government provides relative insulation from economic cycles — has reduced political pressure to cut taxes for revenue competitiveness. For workers who can choose between Virginia and North Carolina for similar jobs, NC's improving tax trajectory is a legitimate factor in a long-term financial comparison.

FAQ

Does Virginia have local income taxes?

Virginia cities and counties do not levy separate local income taxes. Your 5.75% top rate is your complete Virginia income tax obligation — no additional city tax in Arlington, Alexandria, Richmond, or Virginia Beach. This differs from Maryland, where the county piggyback tax adds meaningfully to the state rate.

Is $90,000 a good salary in Northern Virginia?

It's a real salary that supports a genuine professional life, but it doesn't afford the lifestyle that the same salary would deliver in most of the rest of the country. The typical financial strategy at $90,000 in NoVA is to maximize pre-tax savings, keep housing as a modest share of income (which often means outer suburbs or roommates), and build toward the $110,000–$130,000 range where the cost-of-living math becomes more favorable.

Is $90,000 a good salary in Richmond, Roanoke, or other Virginia cities?

Yes — meaningfully so. Outside the NoVA corridor, $90,000 places you well above the local median income in most Virginia cities, with housing costs that allow genuine financial progress: retirement savings, emergency fund building, and a quality lifestyle without the financial stress that characterizes the same salary in NoVA or DC proper.
