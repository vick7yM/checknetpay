$80,000 Salary in Washington State: 2026 Take-Home and the WA Cares Reality

Washington State's no-income-tax status makes it one of the most paycheck-efficient states in the US. On $80,000 in 2026, your standard take-home is $2,504.23 per bi-weekly paycheck. Factor in the WA Cares Fund (the state's long-term care payroll deduction) and it adjusts to approximately $2,487.00 — still well above what most taxing states deliver. Here's the complete picture, including what other calculators frequently miss.

Your Washington State Paycheck: 2026 Full Breakdown

$80,000 across 26 pay periods is $3,076.92 gross per paycheck. Washington adds no state income tax on wages:

📊 $80,000 Salary — Washington State — Single — Bi-weekly (2026)
  • Gross pay per paycheck: $3076.92
  • Federal income tax: -$337.31 (11.0% effective rate)
  • Social Security: -$190.77 (6.2%)
  • Medicare: -$44.62 (1.45%)
  • Washington State income tax on wages: $0 (no wage income tax 🎉)
  • Net take-home: $2504.23 per paycheck
💡 WA Cares Fund Adjustment: Most Washington workers subject to the WA Cares Fund pay 0.58% of gross wages. On $80,000, that's $464/year ($17.85/paycheck), reducing your real net to approximately $2,487.00 per paycheck. If you obtained a WA Cares exemption with private LTC insurance before the November 2021 deadline, your take-home remains $2,504.23. Check your pay stub for a "WA Cares" or "LTC" deduction line.

Federal Tax at $80,000: 22% Bracket in Play

Taxable income: $63,900 ($80,000 minus $16,100 standard deduction). Three brackets:

  • 10% on first $12,400: $1,240.00
  • 12% on $12,400–$50,400: $4,560.00
  • 22% on $50,400–$63,900: $2,970.00
  • Annual federal tax: $8,770 ($337.31/paycheck, 11.0% effective)

The 22% marginal rate at $80,000 means pre-tax contributions create strong immediate returns. A 401(k) contribution of $4,000 saves $880 in federal tax — 22 cents on every dollar. Washington has no state income tax to additionally save on, but the federal savings alone are compelling.

What $80,000 Buys Across Washington State

Seattle

Seattle is the economic epicenter and carries the state's highest cost of living. One-bedroom apartments in walkable neighborhoods (Capitol Hill, Fremont, Ballard, Queen Anne, South Lake Union) run $2,000–$2,800. Further out (Beacon Hill, Columbia City, Rainier Beach, Crown Hill) offers $1,600–$2,000. The Link Light Rail expansion has opened more affordable neighborhoods with reasonable downtown commute times.

At $80,000 ($5,008/month take-home, or $4,974 with WA Cares), Seattle solo living is tight in premium areas but genuinely workable in outer neighborhoods. Seattle's tech salary compression means $80,000 is roughly the 25th percentile for tech workers — many co-workers earn dramatically more, which can create lifestyle comparison pressure. Focus on your own financial trajectory.

Bellevue / Redmond / Kirkland

The Eastside tech corridor (home to Microsoft HQ, Amazon offices, and hundreds of tech companies) commands even higher rents than Seattle — one-bedrooms in Bellevue City Center or Redmond's Overlake area run $2,100–$2,900. At $80,000, the Eastside is genuinely difficult for solo living. Most workers at this salary level on the Eastside have roommates, live further east (Sammamish, Issaquah), or commute from Tacoma or Everett.

Tacoma

The most financially sensible choice for workers willing to commute or work remotely. One-bedrooms in Stadium District, Proctor, and South End run $1,200–$1,700. Sounder commuter rail to Seattle takes 55–65 minutes and costs about $200/month for a monthly pass. Your $4,974 take-home against $1,400 rent in Tacoma leaves $3,574 for everything else — a genuinely comfortable margin that's impossible in Seattle proper at this salary.

Everett

North of Seattle, Everett has grown its own identity beyond Boeing manufacturing (though Boeing remains important). One-bedrooms run $1,300–$1,700. Swift Bus Rapid Transit and Sounder train provide commute options. At $80,000, Everett is another strong value play on the Puget Sound — more affordable than Seattle with improving transit connectivity.

Spokane

Eastern Washington's largest city is a different world economically. One-bedrooms run $900–$1,300. Healthcare (Providence, MultiCare), education (WSU, Gonzaga), and logistics are key employers. At $80,000 in Spokane, you're earning well above the local median ($52,000 household income) and can build substantial savings aggressively. For remote workers, Spokane offers the no-income-tax advantage of Washington plus dramatically lower cost of living than the Puget Sound.

The Oregon Commuter Strategy: Is It Worth It?

Many workers in the Portland metro live in Washington (Vancouver, Camas, Washougal) and commute to Oregon-based jobs. This strategy captures Washington's no-income-tax status while accessing Oregon's job market. At $80,000, the Oregon income tax savings are approximately $7,280/year — roughly $607/month.

The practical considerations: Vancouver housing has become more expensive as this strategy has become well-known, eroding some of the cost-of-living advantage. The I-5 and I-205 bridge crossings into Portland are congested — commute times of 30–90 minutes are common depending on direction and time of day. For workers who can flex their schedule or partially work remotely, the tax savings justify the commute. For workers in time-sensitive roles requiring early morning Portland presence, the commute may not be worth it.

Washington's Capital Gains Tax at $80,000

As noted in our $70,000 Washington article: the 7% capital gains tax enacted in 2022 applies to long-term capital gains above $250,000/year and does not affect W-2 wages at any level. At $80,000 in wages, your Washington tax exposure is $0 on income — and only WA Cares Fund deductions if applicable. The capital gains tax is irrelevant to your paycheck calculation.

FAQ

Does Seattle have a city income tax?

No. Seattle's 2017 attempt to create a city income tax was struck down by the courts. No Washington city currently levies a personal income tax. Your pay stub is free of any local income tax line regardless of where in Washington you work.

How does the WA Cares exemption work if I already have private LTC insurance?

The window to apply for a WA Cares exemption with private LTC insurance closed November 1, 2021. Workers who obtained qualifying private coverage before that date and applied for exemption are permanently exempt. If you missed the window, you generally cannot opt out (certain exceptions exist for out-of-state residents, non-immigrant visa holders, and veterans with VA care). Contact Washington's Employment Security Department if you believe you qualify for an exemption category.

Is $80,000 enough to buy a home in Washington State?

Outside the Seattle metro: potentially, with the right down payment. In Tacoma ($380,000 median), Spokane ($320,000 median), and many Eastern Washington markets, $80,000 salary can support homeownership with a 10–20% down payment and manageable monthly payments. In Seattle ($750,000+ median) or Bellevue ($900,000+ median), $80,000 solo is insufficient for most conventional home purchases in 2026.
