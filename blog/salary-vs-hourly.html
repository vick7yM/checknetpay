Salary vs. Hourly Pay: What's the Real Difference After Tax?

A $50,000 salary and $24/hour look like the same money — until overtime, schedule variability, and benefits enter the picture. Here's what actually changes your take-home pay.

It's Not Just About the Number

A $50,000 salary and a $24-an-hour job both work out to roughly the same annual gross pay (assuming 40 hours/week) — but how that pay reaches your bank account, and how much you actually take home, can differ in important ways once taxes and overtime enter the picture.

💡 Key idea: Salaried pay is the same every period regardless of hours worked. Hourly pay is hours × rate, plus 1.5× your rate for any hours over 40 in a week (overtime).

How Salary Pay Works

A salary is a fixed yearly amount divided evenly across your pay periods. If you're paid bi-weekly (26 paychecks/year), a $52,000 salary means exactly $2,000 gross per paycheck — every single time, whether you worked 38 hours or 45 hours that week.

Most (but not all) salaried positions are classified as "exempt" under the Fair Labor Standards Act, meaning they are not entitled to overtime pay, no matter how many hours they work.

How Hourly Pay Works

Hourly employees are paid for every hour actually worked. If you work 35 hours one week and 45 the next, your gross pay will differ between those two paychecks. Critically, hours over 40 in a single week are paid at 1.5× your normal rate — this is overtime, and it's required by law for most hourly ("non-exempt") workers.

Example: $50,000 Salary vs. $24/Hour

📊 Annual Gross Pay Comparison — 40 Hours/Week
  • $50,000 salary ÷ 52 weeks = $961.54/week
  • $24/hour × 40 hours = $960.00/week
  • Nearly identical at exactly 40 hours/week

But the moment overtime enters the picture, the hourly worker can come out ahead:

📊 Same Week, But With 5 Hours of Overtime
  • Salaried worker (exempt): still $961.54 — no extra pay for the extra 5 hours
  • Hourly worker: 40 × $24 + 5 × $36 (1.5×) = $960 + $180 = $1,140

How Taxes Differ

Taxes themselves don't care whether your income is "salary" or "hourly" — federal and state income tax, plus FICA, are calculated the same way on whatever you actually earn in a pay period. The difference is that an hourly worker's gross pay can vary week to week, so their tax withholding will vary too, while a salaried worker's withholding is typically the same every paycheck.

Other Differences Worth Knowing

  • Benefits — salaried positions more often come with full benefits packages (health insurance, paid time off, retirement matching)
  • Schedule flexibility — salaried roles often have more flexibility around when work gets done
  • Job security in slow periods — hourly workers may see reduced hours (and pay) during slow business periods; salaried pay stays constant

Which Is "Better"?

Neither is universally better — it depends on your situation. If you regularly work overtime, hourly pay with overtime can result in significantly higher take-home pay than an "equivalent" salary. If your hours are stable and you value predictable income and benefits, salary may suit you better.

Whichever way you're paid, your pay stub breaks down exactly where that money goes — from gross pay to net pay.
