$60,000 Salary in Massachusetts: 2026 Take-Home Pay After Taxes

Massachusetts has a reputation for high taxes, but the reality at $60,000 is more nuanced. The state's flat 5% income tax is genuinely simple to understand — and at this income level, it costs you about $115 per paycheck compared to a no-tax state. Your 2026 take-home: $1,822.69 every two weeks, or $47,390 per year. Here's the full picture.

Your Complete 2026 Paycheck Breakdown

A $60,000 salary divided across 26 bi-weekly payments is $2,307.69 gross per check. Four deductions reduce that — three federal, one state:

📊 $60,000 Salary — Massachusetts — Single — Bi-weekly (2026)
  • Gross pay per paycheck: $2,307.69
  • Federal income tax: -$193.08 (8.4% effective rate)
  • Social Security: -$143.08 (6.2%)
  • Medicare: -$33.46 (1.45%)
  • Massachusetts state income tax: -$115.38 (5.00% flat rate)
  • Net take-home: $1,822.69 per paycheck
💡 Massachusetts' flat tax explained: Since 2020, Massachusetts has taxed all wage income at a single flat rate — currently 5% for 2026. There are no brackets, no deductions to calculate, no income phase-outs. You earn $60,000, you pay 5% on $60,000: $3,000/year, or $115.38 per paycheck. The math is unusually transparent for a state income tax.

Breaking Down Every Deduction

Federal Income Tax: $193.08 per paycheck

Your federal taxable income is $43,900 ($60,000 minus the $16,100 standard deduction). This travels through two brackets: 10% on the first $12,400 ($1,240) and 12% on the remaining $31,500 ($3,780). Total annual federal tax: $5,020. Divided by 26 pay periods: $193.08.

Your effective federal rate of 8.4% is meaningfully lower than your marginal rate of 12%, because the 10% bracket and the untaxed standard deduction pull the average down.

Social Security: $143.08 per paycheck

The Social Security tax is 6.2% of every dollar of wages, regardless of income level (up to a $184,500 cap). On $60,000, that's $3,720 per year. Social Security is paying into a benefit you'll eventually receive — the average 2026 Social Security retirement benefit is roughly $1,900/month, though your actual benefit depends on your earnings history over 35 years.

Medicare: $33.46 per paycheck

Medicare takes 1.45% of all wages, with no annual cap. On $60,000, that's $870/year. Unlike Social Security, Medicare has no maximum — earners above $200,000 (single filers) pay an additional 0.9% surcharge, but that doesn't apply at this income level.

Massachusetts State Tax: $115.38 per paycheck

The 5% flat rate applied to your full $60,000 gross salary produces $3,000/year in state taxes. Massachusetts does not offer a state standard deduction for wage income in the traditional sense — the 5% applies to the full gross. However, there are some deductions available on your annual Massachusetts return (rental deduction, MBTA pass deduction, certain charitable contributions) that can modestly reduce your tax bill at filing time.

Massachusetts vs. No-Income-Tax States: Putting $3,000 in Context

The honest comparison: at $60,000, the Massachusetts state income tax costs you $3,000 per year — exactly $115.38 per paycheck — compared to living in Texas, Florida, or Nevada.

Whether that $3,000 is "worth it" depends on what you're buying. Massachusetts spends more per capita on public education than nearly any other state. MBTA commuter rail and subway access in the Boston metro can genuinely replace car ownership (saving $5,000–$10,000/year in car costs and insurance). Massachusetts Healthcare Connector provides subsidized coverage options, and the state's public university system is well-regarded.

If you work in Boston and use the T instead of owning a car, you likely recoup the state income tax cost many times over. If you live in a rural part of the state and drive everywhere, the calculus is different.

What $60,000 Looks Like Across Massachusetts

Boston Proper

Boston is expensive. One-bedroom apartments in Back Bay, South End, or Beacon Hill run $2,500–$3,500/month. Even in Jamaica Plain, Allston, or Brighton (historically student/young professional neighborhoods), expect $1,800–$2,400. Your $3,645 monthly take-home (two paychecks) does not cover a solo one-bedroom in core Boston at any reasonable housing-cost-to-income ratio. Most $60,000 earners in Boston either have roommates, live in outer neighborhoods, or live outside the city and commute.

Cambridge / Somerville

Similar story to Boston proper, driven up by proximity to MIT, Harvard, and Kendall Square's biotech cluster. One-bedrooms regularly exceed $2,400.

Worcester

Worcester is the practical choice for many $60,000 earners who work remotely or commute to Boston occasionally. One-bedrooms run $1,200–$1,600, and the city has invested significantly in its downtown. Commuter rail to South Station Boston takes about 65–90 minutes. At $60,000, Worcester is genuinely livable.

Springfield

Western Massachusetts's largest city is dramatically more affordable — one-bedrooms in the $900–$1,300 range. If your job allows for full remote work or is Springfield-based, $60,000 in Springfield provides a solid middle-class life by any measure.

Suburbs (Framingham, Lowell, Brockton, Quincy)

The suburbs offer the middle path: one-bedrooms at $1,500–$1,900, reasonable commute options via commuter rail, and more square footage per dollar. Many $60,000 earners in the Boston metro find this the sustainable option.

The Millionaires Tax: Does It Affect You?

In 2022, Massachusetts voters approved a 4% surtax on annual income above $1 million — nicknamed the "Fair Share Amendment." At $60,000, this does not affect you in any way. Your state tax rate is 5%, full stop. The surtax only kicks in on income exceeding $1 million and is estimated to affect fewer than 0.1% of Massachusetts filers. It's worth knowing for context, but it has no bearing on a $60,000 paycheck.

Maximizing Your Take-Home in Massachusetts

Unlike some states with complex deduction systems, Massachusetts's flat tax means pre-tax contributions to a 401(k) or HSA reduce your federal taxable income but generally do not reduce your Massachusetts taxable income for wage earners (Massachusetts conforms to federal law on 401(k) pre-tax treatment for the state return — this is worth confirming with a tax professional as state rules occasionally change). The federal tax savings alone still make pre-tax contributions highly worthwhile at the 12% federal bracket.

  • 5% 401(k) contribution ($3,000/year): saves $360 in federal tax, reduces monthly take-home by ~$220
  • Health insurance premiums (pre-tax): reduces federal taxable income dollar for dollar
  • MBTA pass deduction: Massachusetts allows a deduction for public transit costs above $150/month — useful if you're a heavy T user

FAQ

Is Massachusetts income tax higher than the national average?

At 5% flat, Massachusetts is roughly in the middle of the pack for states that tax income. Pennsylvania is 3.07%, Illinois 4.95%, Colorado 4.4%. New York's effective rate at $60,000 is around 5.5%–6%. California's is lower at this income level due to its progressive structure. Massachusetts's flat 5% is not exceptional — it just feels high because Boston is expensive and many people conflate overall cost of living with tax burden.

Does Massachusetts have a city income tax?

No. Unlike New York City (which layers its own income tax on top of NY state tax), no Massachusetts city or town levies an additional income tax. Your 5% flat rate is your only Massachusetts income tax obligation, regardless of whether you work in Boston, Worcester, or Pittsfield.
